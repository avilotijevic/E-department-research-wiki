# Neurostimulation

Neurostimulation is a method to temporarily and non-invasively modulate brain activity. The modulatory effect on the brain can be compared to behavioral or other physiological outputs (EEG, eye tracking). If neurostimulation changes these outputs, it provides causal evidence for this brain area/process being involved in that particular function 

## Types of Neurostimulation

Neurostimulation can be broadly divided into magnetic and electrical stimulation (also ultrasound, but this is not present in Groningen). Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) and transcranial electrical stimulation (tES). Each has various approaches and settings that can be useful for answering different questions.

- **TMS**
  - Single pulse TMS
    To measure excitability of the brain by combining with EMG or EEG
    To interrupt (inhibit) a process
  - Paired pulse TMS
    To investigate inhibitory or excitatory circuits
  - Repetitive TMS
    To induce a longer lasting effect (single session up to 60 min, multiple sessions =  longer effect)
    Used in research and clinical interventions
- **tES**
  - Transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS)
    To slightly increase or decrease firing in a particular brain region (excitation or inhibition)
  - Transcranial alternating current stimulation (tACS)
    To modulate neural oscillations
  - Transcranial random noise stimulation (tRNS)
    To add noise to the brain, which can elevate certain function through stochastic resonance

In this department, neurostimulation is commonly combined with:
- Behavioral tasks
- Eye tracking
- EEG
- EMG
- With each other (tACS with TMS for example)
Note: neurostimulation by itself is only interventional, it has no outcome measure by itself. So it needs to combine with something at any time to address a research question.

Please note that while tES is portable and can be used in any lab, TMS is more difficult to move and will likely remain in the same lab.

## Exclusion criteria 
- Non-negotiable: 
  Epilepsy
  Any kind of electric device in body (like a pace maker)
  Metallic plates in head (fillings, other dental work, and piercings are all fine). 
  For tES only: skin disease or tattoos near the stimulation electrodes.

- Depending on the research study and approach:
  Neurological and psychiatric disorders
  Pregnancy
  Pharmacological or recreational drug use

## Participant preparation TMS

TMS will be scary to many participants. However, it is non-invasive and safe. Ensure them of this by:
- Confirm again with the participant that they do not have any of the exclusion criteria.
- Show them the TMS coil, apply a pulse in the air, to let them hear the sound.
- If you feel it helps, apply TMS on your own arm, showing that it will cause a little twitch
- Start applying TMS on the participant at a low intensity (30-40% of MSO would be a good start). After first pulse ask them if they felt anything and if so how it felt.
- Gradually increase intensity
- No other preparation is required.

## Typical approaches with TMS

To determine stimulation intensity, we need a reference. Typically motor thresholds are used as reference. Apply EMG to the FDI (or alternative) hand muscle.
Stimulate the motor region. Move around to find the region with the strongest response.
Use a staircase method to find the minimal intensity needed to still evoke a response of >0.05 mV
Multiply this intensity with a certain factor for your experiment (typically 120% of MT).
Apply TMS during a task/measurement (e.g., measure excitability, or disrupt) or, do a pre-test and post-test, with TMS in between (e.g. interventional with rTMS).


## TMS parameters
- Single pulse
  Intensity, expressed in % maximum stimulator output (MSO)
- Paired pulse
  Intensity for each pulse
  interpulse interval
- RTMS (cannot be used on the Magstim 200)
  Intensity
  Interpulse interval

## Participant preparation tES
 - Start by identifying where the electrodes need to be placed. You can use electric field modeling (like SimNIBS) to calculate optimal stimulation area. Once the participant is in the chair, identify the region using an EEG cap or measuring type.
- Mark the stimulation regions with a marker or other removable identifier.
- Clean the area with alcohol or NuPrep. Importantly, if you use NuPrep (which has some minor conductive particles in it) make sure it is fully removed, by using a moist and dry towel to rub it out. Using NuPrep followed by a wet and dry towel is the best approach, but is cumbersome.
- Electrodes need to have conductive gel, there are two ways:
- Use adhesive Ten20 gel directly on the rubber electrode (a layer of approx 1mm). This can be directly placed on the participants head. As it is adhesive it will stick (if you press well enough)
- Place the electrodes in a moist sponge and apply EEG on the sponge. This can be placed on the participants head. As this will not stick, you need something else to hold the electrodes in place (EEG cap, bathing cap, rubber bands
- When placing the electrodes make sure to get hairs to the side as much as possible. Use hair clips for people with longer hair (and dont forget to remove the hair clips when stimulating
- Set your parameters.
- Let the participant feel the stimulation at a lower intensity before moving to the intended intensity

## Typical approaches with tES
Use tES online or offline, meaning that you apply it while participants perform a task. Or you do a pretest - tES - posttest approach

## tES parameters
- tDCS:
  Intensity
  Duration

- tACS
  Intensity
  Duration
  Frequency	
  Ramp up/down duration
  Other parameters relating to phase that are rarely used
- tRNS
  Same as tACS
