# Experimental methods

The Experimental Psychology department provides several laboratory methods for studying perception, cognition, and behavior. These methods allow researchers to measure neural, physiological, and behavioral responses during experimental tasks.

Each method requires specific laboratory setups and preparation procedures. Detailed manuals for each method are provided on the linked pages below.

---

## Available methods

### → [Eye tracking](eyetracking.md)

Eye tracking is used to measure **gaze position, eye movements, and pupil size** during experiments.  
In our department, eye tracking experiments are typically conducted using **EyeLink 1000 systems**.

This method is commonly used in studies of:

- visual attention  
- perception  
- reading and decision making  
- pupillometry and cognitive load

---

### → [EEG](eeg.md)

Electroencephalography (EEG) measures **electrical brain activity** from the scalp with high temporal resolution.

EEG allows researchers to study:

- neural responses to stimuli  
- event-related potentials (ERPs)  
- cognitive processing dynamics  

EEG experiments can be combined with behavioral tasks and other physiological measurements.

---

### → [Neurostimulation](neurostimulation.md)

Neurostimulation methods allow researchers to **modulate neural activity** non-invasively in order to investigate causal brain–behavior relationships.

These techniques are used to study:

- brain plasticity  
- causal contributions of specific brain areas  
- neural mechanisms underlying cognition and perception

---

## Choosing a method

The appropriate method depends on the research question and the type of measurements required. In many cases, these methods can also be **combined with behavioral experiments programmed in OpenSesame**.

Before running an experiment, make sure to:

- read the relevant method manual  
- verify that the appropriate lab and equipment are available  
- test the experimental setup before starting data collection
